    Lesson 5: CNF and DNF

    Learning outcomes:

    After completing this lesson you should be able to:

    • recognise whether a formula is in CNF or DNF or both or neither
    • convert a given propositional formula into CNF by using the rewrite rules given in the lesson
    • convert a given propositional formula into DNF by using the rewrite rules given in the lesson
    • convert a given propositional formula into both principal CNF and principal DNF.

    Any propositional formula will be logically equivalent to many different formulas. For example, \(p \vee \neg q\) is equivalent to \(\neg (\neg p \wedge q)\) as well as to \((p \wedge \neg q) \vee (p \wedge q) \vee (\neg p \wedge \neg q)\) as well as to \((\neg q \vee p) \wedge (\neg q \vee q)\).

    In automated reasoning it is often useful to use formulas which have a special format. Two of these formats are introduced here: the Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF) and the Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF). Although we will only use the CNF in detail later, it is instructive to understand how CNF and DNF are conceptually, in an informal sense, 'mirror images' of each other. This duality could be discussed in a precise formal way, but we do not need it in this module.

    Literals, elementary sums and products

    We use the following terminology:

    • A variable or the negation of a variable is called a literal.
    • A formula that is a conjunction of literals is called an elementary product. For example \(p \wedge q \wedge \neg p\) is an elementary product. More examples are \(p\) and \(p \wedge \neg p\).
    • A formula that is a disjunction of literals is called an elementary sum. For example \(p \vee q \vee \neg p\) is an elementary sum. More examples are \(p\) and \(p \vee \neg p\).

    Note that from now we do not usually need to distinguish equivalent formulas which just have disjunctions bracketed in different ways. For example: \(p \vee (q \vee \neg p)\) and \((p \vee q) \vee \neg p\). We allow ourselves to write \(p \vee q \vee \neg p\) instead, and similarly for conjunctions.

    We introduce the following normal forms, which are special formats or 'shapes' that a formula may have. A formula may have both or neither of these two forms, or it can have just one of them.

    • A formula is said to be in conjunctive normal form if it is a conjunction of elementary sums.
    • A formula is said to be in disjunctive normal form if it is a disjunction of elementary products.
    • We often abbreviate these to CNF and DNF respectively.

    Examples

    • \((p \vee q \vee \neg q) \wedge (p \vee \neg r) \wedge (r \vee q)\) is in conjunctive normal form
    • \((p \wedge q \wedge \neg q) \vee (p \wedge \neg r) \vee (r \wedge q)\) is in disjunctive normal form
    • \((p \vee (q \wedge r)) \wedge (q \vee p) \wedge (p \vee \neg q)\) is not in CNF nor in DNF
    • \(p \vee \neg p \vee p \vee \neg p\) is both in CNF and DNF
    • \(p \to q\) is not in CNF or DNF, but it is equivalent to \(\neg p \vee q\) which is both.

    Finding equivalent CNF and DNF for formulas

    Every formula is equivalent to one formula in CNF and also to one formula in DNF. In fact there will be many different possible CNF or DNF equivalents for a formula.

    Method for CNF

    The method to find an equivalent CNF for any formula is to apply the following rewrite rules:

    1. \(p \to q\) is replaced by \(\neg p \vee q\)
    2. \(\neg(p \vee q)\) is replaced by \(\neg p \wedge \neg q\)
    3. \(\neg(p \wedge q)\) is replaced by \(\neg p \vee \neg q\)
    4. \(\neg \neg p\) is replaced by \(p\).
    5. \(p \vee (q \wedge r)\) is replaced by \((p \vee q) \wedge (p \vee r)\).
    6. \((p \wedge q) \vee r\) is replaced by \((p \vee r) \wedge (q \vee r)\).

    The rules are applied in the order (1) then ((2) and (3)) then (4) then ((5) and (6))

    Example

    To find a CNF for \(p \to (\neg(q \to \neg r) \wedge s)\) we can use these steps:

    \(\begin{array}{l} p \to (\neg(q \to \neg r) \wedge s) & \mathsf{Starting~ formula}\\[1ex] \neg p \vee (\neg(q \to \neg r) \wedge s) & \mathsf{using~ rule~ 1.}\\[1ex] \neg p \vee (\neg(\neg q \vee \neg r) \wedge s) & \mathsf{using~ rule~ 1.}\\[1ex] \neg p \vee ((\neg\neg q \wedge \neg \neg r) \wedge s) & \mathsf{using~ rule~ 2.}\\[1ex] \neg p \vee ( (q \wedge r) \wedge s) & \mathsf{using~ rule~ 4~ twice.}\\[1ex] (\neg p \vee (q \wedge r)) \wedge (\neg p \vee s) & \mathsf{using~ rule~ 5.}\\[1ex] ((\neg p \vee q) \wedge (\neg p \vee r)) \wedge (\neg p \vee s) & \mathsf{using~ rule~ 5~ again.}\\[1ex] (\neg p \vee q) \wedge (\neg p \vee r) \wedge (\neg p \vee s) & \mathsf{just~ removing~ unnecessary~ parentheses.}\\[1ex] \end{array}\)

    Method for DNF

    The method to find an equivalent DNF for any formula is to apply the following rewrite rules:

    1. \(p \to q\) is replaced by \(\neg p \vee q\)
    2. \(\neg(p \vee q)\) is replaced by \(\neg p \wedge \neg q\)
    3. \(\neg \neg p\) is replaced by \(p\).
    4. \(p \wedge (q \vee r)\) is replaced by \((p \wedge q) \vee (p \wedge r)\).
    5. \((p \vee q) \wedge r\) is replaced by \((p \wedge r) \vee (q \wedge r)\).

    The rules are applied in the order (1) then ((2) and (3)) then (4) then ((5) and (6))

    Example

    To find a DNF for \(p \to ((q \to \neg r) \wedge s)\) we can follow these steps.

    You should check you can tell which rule is used at each step.

    \(\begin{array}{l} p \to ((q \to \neg r) \wedge s)\\[1ex] \neg p \vee ((q \to \neg r) \wedge s)\\[1ex] \neg p \vee ((\neg q \vee \neg r) \wedge s)\\[1ex] \neg p \vee ( (\neg q \wedge s) \vee (\neg r \wedge s))\\[1ex] \neg p \vee (\neg q \wedge s) \vee (\neg r \wedge s)\\ \end{array}\)

    Principal DNF and CNF

    In general there are lots of different formulas in DNF which are all logically equivalent to a given formula. The same is true of CNF. To get an essentially unique normal form, we need to use the principal DNF or principal CNF. These are 'essentially unique' in the sense that they only differ in the ordering of the elementary sums or products involved.

    To find the principal DNF

    These examples show that a given formula can have many logical equivalents in DNF.

    Examples

    All these four DNF are equivalent to each other

    \(\begin{array}{l} p \vee (q \wedge r) \vee \neg r\\[1ex] (p \wedge q \wedge r) \vee (p \wedge \neg q \wedge r) \vee (p \wedge q \wedge \neg r) \vee (p \wedge \neg q \wedge \neg r) \vee (q \wedge r) \vee \neg r\\[1ex] (p \wedge \neg q \wedge r) \vee (q \wedge r) \vee \neg r\\[1ex] p \vee (p \wedge q \wedge r) \vee (\neg p \wedge q \wedge r)\vee \neg r \end{array}\)

    It is possible to find a DNF which is essentially unique for any formula. This is called the principal DNF and is easy to understand if we think about the connection between the conjunctions appearing and the rows in a truth table.

    A DNF for a formula tells us which rows in its truth table give the result true. For \(\neg p \vee (\neg q \wedge s) \vee (\neg r \wedge s)\) we can see that a row in the table will give \(T\) when

    1. \(p\) is \(F\), or
    2. \(q\) is \(F\) and \(s\) is \(T\), or
    3. \(r\) is \(F\) and \(s\) is \(T\).

    We can express this diagrammatically; rows of other forms have \(F\) in final column.

    \(\begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline p&q&r&s&\neg p \vee (\neg q \wedge s) \vee (\neg r \wedge s)\\ \hline F & ? & ? & ? & T\\ \hline ?&F&?&T&T\\ \hline ?&?&F&T&T\\ \hline \end{array}\)

    In this example none of the conjunctions involves all of the four variables in the formula. We can see that if it did then it would correspond to a unique row in the truth table. For example, \(p \wedge \neg q \wedge r \wedge \neg s\) corresponds to the row in which \(p\) and \(r\) are assigned \(T\) and in which \(q\) and \(s\) are assigned \(F\). The principal DNF for a formula is just the disjunction of all the conjunctions involving all the variables for which the corresponding row in the truth table has \(T\) as its result.

    Finding principal DNF of \((p \to q) \to r\) from the truth table.

    \(\begin{array}{cr|c|c|c|c|c|l} &&p & q & r & p \to q & (p \to q) \to r\\ \hline p \wedge q \wedge r & \Longrightarrow & T & T & T & T & T & \Longleftarrow \\ && T & T & F & T & F &\\ p \wedge \neg q \wedge r & \Longrightarrow & T & F & T & F & T &\Longleftarrow \\ p \wedge \neg q \wedge \neg r & \Longrightarrow & T & F & F & F & T &\Longleftarrow \\ \neg p \wedge q \wedge r & \Longrightarrow & F & T & T & T & T &\Longleftarrow \\ && F & T & F & T & F &\\ \neg p \wedge \neg q \wedge r & \Longrightarrow & F & F & T & T & T &\Longleftarrow \\ &&F & F & F & T & F &\\ \hline \end{array}\)

    The answer of the following formula:

    \[ (p \wedge q \wedge r) \vee (p \wedge \neg q \wedge r) \vee (p \wedge \neg q \wedge \neg r) \vee (\neg p \wedge q \wedge r) \vee (\neg p \wedge \neg q \wedge r).\]

    Writing out the full truth table is not the only way to find the principal DNF. We can work from a DNF which is not principal which we obtained by rewriting the formula.

    Example

    Find the principal DNF of \((p \to q) \to r\) without using a truth table.

    First we find a DNF by rewriting: \(\left\{\begin{array}{l} (p \to q) \to r \\[1ex] \neg (p \to q) \vee r \\[1ex] \neg (\neg p \vee q) \vee r \\[1ex] (\neg \neg p \wedge \neg q) \vee r \\[1ex] ( p \wedge \neg q) \vee r \end{array}\right.\)

    Second: we expand each of the conjunctions so it becomes a disjunction of conjunctions where all the variables appear in each conjunction.

    \(( p \wedge \neg q)\) becomes \(( p \wedge \neg q \wedge r)\vee ( p \wedge \neg q \wedge \neg r)\)

    \(r\) becomes \((p \wedge q \wedge r) \vee (\neg p \wedge q \wedge r) \vee (p \wedge \neg q \wedge r) \vee (\neg p \wedge \neg q \wedge r)\)

    The second step could alternatively be done by rewriting \(( p \wedge \neg q)\) to \(( p \wedge \neg q) \wedge (r \vee \neg r)\) and rewriting \(r\) to \((p \vee \neg p) \wedge (q \vee \neg q) \wedge r\) and then rewriting to DNF using the distributive laws.

    Third: write down the terms we have, throwing out any duplicates:

    \(( p \wedge \neg q \wedge r)\vee ( p \wedge \neg q \wedge \neg r) \vee (p \wedge q \wedge r) \vee (\neg p \wedge q \wedge r) \vee (\neg p \wedge \neg q \wedge r)\)

    Observe that the principal DNF of a tautology involving \(n\) variables will have \(2^n\) conjunctions, as we have to pick out every row in the truth table. But the principal DNF of a contradiction would want to pick out none of the rows, so we will just allow \(F\) as a principal DNF. It is correct to think of a disjunction of no terms as false, but if you find the idea confusing it's not important here. In any case, it is difficult to denote an empty disjunction by an empty space, so allowing \(F\) as a formula itself is convenient.

    To find principal CNF

    It is a similar but dual story for the principal CNF. The idea can be summarised as follows, but the example shows how it works.

    Principal DNF:

    • Find all the values of variables for which the formula can be true, and say at least one of these must happen

    Principal CNF:

    • Find all the values of variables for which the formula can be false, and say none of these must happen.

    Example

    Find the principal CNF of \((p \to q) \to r\) from the truth table.

    \[\begin{array}{cr|c|c|c|c|c|l} &&p & q & r & p \to q & (p \to q) \to r\\ \hline & & T & T & T & T & T & \\ \neg p \vee \neg q \vee r &\Longrightarrow & T & T & F & T & F & \Longleftarrow \text{ to get $T$ avoid this row} \\ && T & F & T & F & T & \\ && T & F & F & F & T & \\ && F & T & T & T & T & \\ p \vee \neg q \vee r &\Longrightarrow & F & T & F & T & F & \Longleftarrow \text{ to get $T$ avoid this row} \\ && F & F & T & T & T & \\ p \vee q \vee r &\Longrightarrow &F & F & F & T & F & \Longleftarrow \text{ to get $T$ avoid this row} \\ \hline \end{array}\]

    The answer is \((\neg p \vee \neg q \vee r) \wedge (p \vee \neg q \vee r ) \wedge (p \vee q \vee r)\)

    Note that, for example, \((\neg p \vee \neg q \vee r)\) is true for the values of the variables in every row except the one indicated. When this one conjunction is true we must have values of variables not corresponding to this false row. All the three conjunctions are true if and only if we have values of variables corresponding to none of the true rows.

    Just as for principal DNF you don't need to construct the truth table; you can take any CNF and add in extra variables.

    Observe that the principal CNF of a contradiction involving \(n\) variables will have \(2^n\) disjunctions, as we have to avoid every row in the truth table. But the principal CNF of a tautology would want to avoid none of the rows, so we will just allow \(T\) as a principal CNF. It is correct to think of a conjunction of no terms as true, but if you find the idea confusing it's not important here.

    A summary of the definitions in CNF and DNF

    This summary is also available as a separate PDF document which highlights the way the concepts come in dual pairs. The summary includes the notions of minterm and maxterm which were not used above.

    Elementary sum

    A propositional logic formula is an elementary sum iff

    1. it is a single literal, or
    2. it consists of two or more literals like this: \(\ell_1 \vee \ell_2 \vee \cdots \vee \ell_m\)

    Examples:

    • \(p\)
    • \(\neg p\)
    • \(p \vee p\)
    • \(p \vee q \vee \neg p \vee q\)
    • \(p \vee \neg q \vee r\)

    Elementary product

    A propositional logic formula is an elementary product iff

    1. it is a single literal, or
    2. it consists of two or more literals like this: \(\ell_1 \wedge \ell_2 \wedge \cdots \wedge \ell_m\)

    Examples:

    • \(p\)
    • \(\neg p\)
    • \(p \wedge p\)
    • \(p \wedge q \wedge \neg p \wedge q\)
    • \(p \wedge \neg q \wedge r\)

    CNF

    A formula is in CNF iff

    1. if is \(T\) (true), or
    2. it is one elementary sum, or
    3. it consists of two or more elementary sums like this \(e_1 \wedge e_2 \wedge \cdots \wedge e_m\)

    Examples:

    • \(T\) (by 1)
    • \(p\) (by 2)
    • \(\neg p\) (by 2)
    • \(p \vee p\) (by 2)
    • \(p \vee q \vee \neg p \vee q\) (by 2)
    • \((p \vee \neg q \vee r) \wedge (p \vee q \vee \neg p)\) (by 3)

    DNF

    A formula is in DNF iff

    1. if is \(F\) (false), or
    2. it is one elementary product, or
    3. it consists of two or more elementary products like this:\ \(e_1 \vee e_2 \vee \cdots \vee e_m\)

    Examples:

    • \(F\) (by 1)
    • \(p\) (by 2)
    • \(\neg p\) (by 2)
    • \(p \wedge p\) (by 2)
    • \(p \vee q \vee \neg p \vee q\) (by 3)
    • \((p \wedge \neg q \wedge r) \vee (p \wedge q \wedge \neg p)\) (by 3)

    Maxterm

    An elementary sum is a maxterm over a given set of variables iff it uses every variable in the set exactly once.

    Examples:

    • \(p\) over \(\{p\}\)
    • \(\neg p\) over \(\{p\}\)
    • \(p \vee q\) over \(\{p,q\}\)
    • \(p \vee q \vee \neg r\) over \(\{p,q,r\}\)

    Non-Examples

    • \(\neg p\) over \(\{p,q\}\)
    • \(p \vee \neg q\) over \(\{p,q,r\}\)

    Minterm

    An elementary product is a minterm over a given set of variables iff it uses every variable in the set exactly once.

    Examples:

    • \(p\) over \(\{p\}\)
    • \(\neg p\) over \(\{p\}\)
    • \(p \wedge q\) over \(\{p,q\}\)
    • \(p \wedge q \wedge \neg r\) over \(\{p,q,r\}\)

    Non-Examples

    • \(\neg p\) over \(\{p,q\}\)
    • \(p \wedge \neg q\) over \(\{p,q,r\}\)

    Principal CNF

    A formula is in principal CNF over a set of variables iff

    1. if is in CNF, and
    2. every elementary sum is a maxterm (over the set)

    Examples:

    • \(T\)
    • \(p\) over \(\{p\}\)
    • \(p \vee q\) over \(\{p,q\}\)
    • \((p \vee q) \wedge (\neg p \vee q)\) over \(\{p,q\}\)
    • \((p \vee \neg q \vee r) \wedge (p \vee q \vee \neg p)\) over \(\{p,q,r\}\)

    Principal DNF

    A formula is in principal DNF over a set of variables iff

    1. if is in DNF, and
    2. every elementary product is a minterm (over the set)

    Examples:

    • \(F\)
    • \(p\) over \(\{p\}\)
    • \(p \wedge q\) over \(\{p,q\}\)
    • \((p \wedge q) \vee (\neg p \wedge q)\) over \(\{p,q\}\)
    • \((p \wedge \neg q \wedge r) \vee (p \wedge q \wedge \neg p)\) over \(\{p,q,r\}\)

    \[ \begin{array}{rc} \mathsf{Truth~ table} & \begin{array}{cccc} TT \mapsto F & TF \mapsto T & FT \mapsto T & FF \mapsto F \end{array} \\[1ex] \mathsf{PCNF}& ( \neg p \vee \neg q) \wedge (p\vee q) \\ \mathsf{PDNF}& ( p \wedge \neg q) \vee (\neg p \wedge q) \end{array}\]
