    Lesson 2: Propositional logic

    This may be revision if you are familiar with this material. It is included here to make sure the notation and terminology is clear as some of this may be different from what you know.

    Learning outcomes:

    After completing this lesson you should be able to:

    • use truth tables to determine whether a statement in propositional logic is a tautology, a contradiction, or a contingency
    • understand the syntax of propositional logic and be able to spot mistakes.

    Propositions

    A proposition is a statement that (perhaps in some context) is either true or false (even if no one knows which).

    Examples and non-examples

    • Today is Monday is a proposition (if we fix when Today is).
    • The room is empty is a proposition (if we know which room).
    • Leeds is in England is a proposition (assuming we agree on the meaning of the places mentioned).
    • Leeds is bigger than London is a proposition. Being a proposition has nothing to do with whether it is true or false
    • Is Leeds in China? is not a proposition, it's a question.
    • \(x = 1\) is a proposition (if we know what \(x\) is).
    • Let \(x =1\) is not a proposition; it's a request or an instruction.
    • x := 1 (in a programming language) is not a proposition; it is a command or an instruction to do something

    A compound proposition is a statement that consists of simpler propositions combined together. For example, The room is empty and the window is broken is made by combining The room is empty with the window is broken. Formal propositional logic models combinations like this. In classical logic the combinations behave in a truth functional way. This means that the truth of a compound proposition depends only on what combination is used and on the truth of the component parts.

    Syntax

    Formal logic has a precise syntax, just like any programming language.

    Propositional logic builds statements out of:

    • Letters (or words) for atomic propositions, often called propositional variables or just variables.
    • The logical connectives
      • \(\wedge\) (and),
      • \(\vee\) (or),
      • \(\neg\) (not),
      • \(\to\) (implies / if – then –),
      • \(\leftrightarrow\) (iff / – if and only if –).
    • Parentheses '(' and ')' to avoid ambiguity.

    The statements in the logic are sometimes called formulas or formulae. These are both acceptable plurals of formula.

    Sometimes, instead of 'formula', people say well formed formula or wff (which you can pronounce 'woof' as in the sound made by a dog) to emphasise that there are strings of symbols, such as

    \[p\; \vee\; \to \;q \;r \;\neg \qquad \text{ or } \qquad (\;(\;p\;(\;q\;(\;(\;\wedge\;\neg \]

    which are (in programming terms) 'syntax errors' and are not 'well formed'.

    The use of single letters for propositional variables saves a lot of effort, but can lead to less intelligible expressions. In computer science, the importance of meaningful identifiers in programming is well known. So, we may sometimes write logical expressions like this:

    \[\mathit{theSunIsShiningToday} \to (\neg \mathit{itIsRaining} \vee \mathit{itIsWarmOutsideToday})\]

    rather than just \(s \to (\neg r \vee w)\).

    It is important not to be misled by imagining that using meaningful names for propositions makes any difference to the logical content. The meaningful names are very useful for humans, but the two logical expressions above would be treated in the same way when processed by software dealing with logic.

    Precedence conventions

    To avoid using too many parentheses we can use conventions about precedence of logical connectives. In the following table a lower number means insert parentheses earlier.

    \[ \begin{array}{lcccccc} \hline \mathsf{Operator} & \neg & \wedge & \vee & \to & \leftrightarrow\\ \mathsf{Precedence} & 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 & 5\\ \hline \end{array} \]

    Examples

    • \(\neg p \vee q \wedge r \to s\) means \(((\neg p) \vee (q \wedge r)) \to s\)
    • \(p \wedge \neg q \to r \vee s \wedge t\) means \((p \wedge (\neg q)) \to (r \vee (s \wedge t))\)
    • \(p \to \neg q \leftrightarrow r \wedge s \vee t\) means \((p \to (\neg q)) \leftrightarrow ((r \wedge s) \vee t)\)

    It is often assumed that binary operators associate to the left so that for example

    • \(p \to q \to r\) means \((p \to q) \to r\) not \(p \to (q \to r)\)
    • \(p \wedge q \wedge r\) means \((p \wedge q) \wedge r\) not \(p \wedge (q \wedge r)\).

    Rather than use all the precedence rules all the time you can add more parentheses than strictly necessary, as follows.

    Suggested use of parentheses and precedence

    • \(\neg\) has highest precedence among propositional connectives
    • \(\vee\) and \(\wedge\) come next, but put in parentheses to distinguish \((p \vee q) \wedge r\) and \(p \vee (q \wedge r)\),
    • \(\to\) and \(\leftrightarrow\) come next, but put in parentheses to distinguish \((p \to q) \leftrightarrow r\) and \(p \to (q \leftrightarrow r)\)
    • put in parentheses to distinguish \((p \to q) \to r\) and \(p \to (q \to r)\); and similarly for all the binary connectives, so that if the difference between \((p \vee q) \vee r\) and \(p \vee (q \vee r)\) is important then write them in this way.

    Semantics

    In classical propositional logic the meaning of the connectives is defined by truth tables as follows. You should have seen these before and be familiar with the process of working out a truth table for any compound proposition.

    Beware: logic can differ from everyday discourse

    From the knowledge representation perspective, some key points to note about the connectives are:

    • \(\vee\) means inclusive or. The formula \(p \vee q\) is true when both \(p\) and \(q\) are true, and also when exactly one is true.
      If you need to say '\(p\) or \(q\), but not both' then you have to say explicitly, such as one of the following.
    \[ (p \vee q) \wedge \neg(p \wedge q) \qquad \qquad (p \wedge \neg q) \vee (q \wedge \neg p)\]

    • \(\wedge\) means and but sometimes the place we include the word in statements can be confusing.
      In the sentence "I bought an apple and an orange", the part "I bought an apple" is a proposition, but "an orange" (which comes after the connecting "and") is not a proposition. In the example the statement is essentially a short form of "I bought an apple and I bought an orange" in which you clearly see two propositions connected together.

    • \(\to\) means material implication. The truth of \(p \to q\) has nothing at all to do with whether \(p\) causes \(q\) or whether there is any relationship between what the propositions represent.

    The expression gets its truth value only from the separate individual truth values of \(p\) and of \(q\).

    This can be confusing because in everyday speech we can use 'if — then —' in other ways. For example, if we model the statement 'if I want to speak Russian then I can speak Russian' in propositional logic as \(w \to s\) where the statement \(w\): 'I want to speak Russian' is false, then the statement as a whole is true. This is the case whether or not \(s\): 'I can speak Russian' holds. Yet, in an everyday situation, someone saying 'if I want to speak Russian then I can speak Russian' who was completely unable to speak Russian at all, might not be regarded as being truthful.

    We will return to this issue when we discuss representation in more detail. The fact that classical propositional logic has these features does not mean it is fundamentally 'wrong'. It is a tool which is capable of representing certain aspects of the world but in the process ignores other aspects. This is found in any kind of modelling process. There are ways to model aspects of the language if — then — that are ignored in classical propositional logic, such as relevance logic, but these are complex and are beyond the scope of this module.

    Truth tables for connectives

    The truth tables are listed here, but you should be familiar with these already.

    \[ \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline p & q & p \wedge q\\ \hline T & T & T \\ T & F & F \\ F & T & F \\ F & F & F \\ \hline \end{array} \qquad \qquad \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline p & q & p \vee q\\ \hline T & T & T \\ T & F & T \\ F & T & T \\ F & F & F \\ \hline \end{array} \qquad \qquad \begin{array}{|c|c|} \hline p & \neg p\\ \hline T & F \\ F & T \\ \hline \end{array}\]
    \[\begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline p & q & p \to q\\ \hline T & T & T \\ T & F & F \\ F & T & T \\ F & F & T \\ \hline \end{array} \qquad \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline p & q & p \leftrightarrow q\\ \hline T & T & T \\ T & F & F \\ F & T & F \\ F & F & T \\ \hline \end{array} \]

    Types of proposition and relationships between propositions

    These types of proposition are important:

    • tautology: This means a proposition that is true under all values given to the variables it contains. For example \(p \vee \neg p\) is a tautology. However, \(p \wedge \neg p\) is not a tautology, and neither is \(p\).

    • contingency: This means a proposition that is true overall when its variables are given some truth values, but false in other cases. For example \(p \vee q\) is a contingency. However neither \(p \vee \neg p\) nor \(p \wedge \neg p\) is a contingency.

    • contradiction: This means a proposition that is false overall whatever truth values its variables are given. For example \(p \wedge \neg p\) is a contradiction.

    A proposition is said to be satisfiable when there is some choice of values for the variables that makes it true. All tautologies and all contingencies are satisfiable. A proposition which is not satisfiable is said to be unsatisfiable.

    Besides propositions individually being of a certain type, we also need to talk about relationships between propositions. We say that a proposition \(\alpha\) logically implies a proposition \(\beta\) if whenever \(\alpha\) evaluates to true, then \(\beta\) does so as well. When this happens we write \(\alpha \vDash \beta\).

    Logical implication is a relationship between propositions, not a new way of constructing propositions. It is similar to the situation when discussing different natural languages. Someone talking in German about two English statements could be describing a relationship between English statements but they would be making a statement in German and not in English.

    The symbol \(\vDash\) is sometimes called a double turnstile when people talk about the symbol itself rather than what it means. A turnstile in non-mathematical contexts is a kind of revolving gate to control a flow of people. In most traditional forms a turnstile revolves about a vertical axis, like the single vertical line in the symbol. When we get to proof we also meet the single turnstile, \(\vdash\). Either of these symbols might just be called turnstile if it causes no ambiguity and, like of lot of mathematical symbols, there is no consistency about how different people may use them in writing about logic.

    Sometimes the relationship of logical implication is called semantic consequence. This is because it depends on the semantics of the statements involved, which in the context of propositional logic means how the truth values of propositions are related. This is different from the notion of proof or syntactic consequence where relationships depend on the syntactic structure of the formulas.

    In reading out the formula \(P \vDash Q\) you can say '\(P\) logically implies \(Q\)' or '\(P\) entails \(Q\)'. An example of someone talking about the \(\vDash\) symbol as opposed to using it would be 'I am using the double turnstile to denote logical implication'.

    For example, the following logical implications hold:

    \[ \neg p \vDash p \to q, \qquad p \wedge q \vDash q \vee \neg (p \wedge r), \qquad p \wedge \neg p \vDash q. \]

    Note in the last example that the statement to the left of the \(\vDash\) symbol is never true. Logical implication is only claiming that when the statement on the left is true, then the statement on the right is true. You may find it helpful to think of this another way: \(\alpha \vDash \beta\) means that it is impossible for \(\alpha\) to be true for some values of the propositional variables and for \(\beta\) to be false for those same values.

    More generally, a logical implication can involve any number of formulas on the left. So that \(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \ldots, \alpha_n \vDash \beta\) asserts that whenever all of the \(\alpha_i\) are true then \(\beta\) is true too. You may find it helpful to think of this another way: \(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \ldots, \alpha_n \vDash \beta\) means that it is impossible for all of the \(\alpha_i\) to be true for some values of the propositional variables and for \(\beta\) to be false for those same values.

    For example the following logical implication holds. You can check this either by drawing up truth tables, or by thinking of all the possible ways to make all of the formulas on the left come out true, and making sure that the formula on the right is true in every one of these cases.

    \[ P \to (Q \vee \neg R),\; P \wedge R,\; Q \to S \vDash Q \wedge S \]

    In the special case that there are no formulas on the left, writing \({ }\vDash \beta\) just means that \(\beta\) is a tautology.

    Truth tables provide a mechanical process

    Truth tables provide a finite procedure which for any formula will say whether it is a tautology or not. More generally, given any claimed logical implication

    \[\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \ldots, \alpha_n \vDash \beta\]

    whether this holds or not can be determined automatically: simply draw up a truth table to find out for what values of the variables involved all of the formulas on the left evaluate to true, and then see whether or not the formula, \(\beta\) on the left is true in all of these cases.

    Propositional logic is very different from other logics that we meet later in that there are only a finite number of ways of interpreting the symbols in any formula. The propositional variables can only be true or false, so if there are \(n\) different variables there are \(2^n\) different interpretations for the formula. Each of these interpretations corresponds to a line in the truth table.
