    Natural deduction

    Learning outcomes:

    After completing this lesson you should be able to:

    • Build natural deduction proofs in tree format for propositional logic using formal rules at every step.
    • Use temporary assumptions in these proofs and discharge them when the rules permit.
    • Check natural deduction proofs for errors.

    Introduction to natural deduction

    In this lesson we study formal proofs set out in a tree format and in a style called natural deduction. It is called natural deduction because the structure of the formal proof is close to the kind of argument that might be made in an informal way – when you are trying to explain the reason for something to somebody else.

    As with any style of proof, natural deduction proofs are used to justify that a conclusion follows from some hypotheses, or in some cases from no hypotheses. In the example studied in lesson 1, the proof justified:

    \[R \rightarrow S, S \rightarrow \neg R \vdash \neg R.\]

    In this example there are two hypotheses: \(R \rightarrow S\) and \(S \rightarrow \neg R\), and a conclusion: \(\neg R\). The key features to watch out for in natural deduction are:

    1. Temporary assumptions can be made which parts of the proof depend on, but which are not needed to justify the ultimate conclusion.
    2. When temporary assumptions are no longer needed they are discharged. In the proof a discharged assumption appears between square brackets \([\;\;]\).
    3. In addition to the modus ponens example in Lesson 1, there are other inference rules. These exist for each logical connective, and come in two flavours, or kinds:
      1. Introduction rules, in which the connective is introduced into the conclusion of the rule
      2. Elimination rules, in which an occurrence of the connective in an antecedent of the rule is eliminated and does not appear in the conclusion of the rule.

    The proof shown in the video is this one:

    \[ \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$S \vee \neg S$} \AXC{$[\neg S]$} \AXC{$[R]$} \AXC{$R \to S$} \BIC{$S$} \BIC{$S \wedge \neg S$} \UIC{$\neg R$} \AXC{$[S]$} \AXC{$S \to \neg R$} \BIC{$\neg R$} \TIC{$\neg R$} \end{prooftree} \]

    The proof shows that \(S \vee \neg S,\; R \to S,\; S \to \neg R \vdash \neg R\).

    The natural deduction rules for each connective

    Each connective has one or more rules to introduce the connective, and one or more rules to eliminate the connective. Each rule has a name which appears to the right of the horizontal line. The name consists of the symbol for the connective followed by \(I\) (Introduction) or \(E\) (Elimination), with possible additional letters to indicate a left hand version (\(\ell\)) or a right hand one (\(r\)). When a rule allows you to discharge an assumption, the name of the rule can carry a subscript number to keep track of which assumption is being discharged by a particular occurrence of a rule.

    The following video explains the rules.

    Download transcript (PDF).

    Rules for AND

    \[ \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{Introduction}\\[2ex] \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$P$} \AXC{$Q$} \RightLabel{$\;\;\wedge I$} \BIC{$P \wedge Q$} \end{prooftree} \end{array} \qquad \qquad \qquad \qquad \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{Elimination}\\[2ex] \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$P \wedge Q$} \RightLabel{$\;\;\wedge Er$} \UIC{$P$} \end{prooftree} \quad \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$P \wedge Q$} \RightLabel{$\;\;\wedge E\ell$} \UIC{$Q$} \end{prooftree} \end{array} \]

    Rules for OR

    \[ \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{Introduction}\\[9ex] \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$P$} \RightLabel{$\;\vee Ir$} \UIC{$P \vee Q$} \end{prooftree} \;\; \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$Q$} \RightLabel{$\;\vee I\ell$} \UIC{$P \vee Q$} \end{prooftree} \end{array} \qquad \qquad \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{Elimination}\\[2ex] \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$A \vee B$} \AXC{$[A]_n$} \noLine \UIC{$\vdots$} \noLine \UIC{$P$} \AXC{$[B]_n$} \noLine \UIC{$\vdots$} \noLine \UIC{$P$} \RightLabel{$\;{\vee}E_n$} \TIC{$P$} \end{prooftree} \end{array} \]

    The elimination rule for \(\vee\) looks complex but comes from the following simple scenario:

    • You know that \(A \vee B\) is true (but not which is true, or if both are)
    • You want to prove \(P\)
    • You can prove \(P\) by assuming \(A\) is true
    • You can prove \(P\) by assuming \(B\) is true
    • Then you are entitled to conclude that \(P\) holds and discharge the temporary assumptions about \(A\) and \(B\).

    Rules for NOT

    \[ \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{Introduction}\\[2ex] \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$[A]_{n}$} \noLine \UIC{$\vdots$} \noLine \UIC{$P \wedge \neg P$} \RightLabel{$\; \neg I_{n}$} \UIC{$\neg A$} \end{prooftree} \end{array} \qquad \qquad \qquad \qquad \qquad \qquad \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{Elimination}\\[2ex] \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$[\neg A]_{n}$} \noLine \UIC{$\vdots$} \noLine \UIC{$P \wedge \neg P$} \RightLabel{$\; \neg E_{n}$} \UIC{$A$} \end{prooftree} \end{array} \]

    The idea behind both the introduction rule and the elimination rule shown above, is the reasoning strategy known as reductio ad absurdum in which an assumption must be rejected because it leads to a contradiction, or, in other words, an absurdity.

    In the introduction rule we start off with an assumption that \(A\) is true, and derive a contradiction, so we conclude \(A\) is false and we can discharge the assumption \(A\). In the elimination rule we start off with an assumption that \(A\) is false, and derive a contradiction, so we conclude \(A\) is true and we can discharge the assumption \(\neg A\).

    Rules for IMPLIES

    \[ \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{Introduction}\\[2ex] \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$[P]_{n}$} \noLine \UIC{$\vdots$} \noLine \UIC{$Q$} \RightLabel{$\; {\to} I_{n}$} \UIC{$P \to Q$} \end{prooftree} \end{array} \qquad \qquad \qquad \qquad \qquad \qquad \begin{array}{c} \mathsf{Elimination}\\[9ex] \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$P$} \AXC{$P \to Q$} \RightLabel{$\; {\to}E$} \BIC{$Q$} \end{prooftree} \end{array} \]

    The elimination rule for \(\to\) is the rule we met in Lesson 1 under the name modus ponens. The introduction rule allows us to assume that \(P\) is true and argue why \(Q\) must then be true, and from that to conclude that \(P \to Q\) holds without the assumption \(P\) which is discharged.

    The introductory examples in complete detail

    Example 1

    Returning now to the example at the start of this lesson, we can now present it in the form below in which each step is labelled by the rule which justifies the step.

    \[ \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$[R]_1$} \AXC{$[R]_1$} \AXC{$R \to S$} \RightLabel{$\; {\to}E$} \BIC{$S$} \AXC{$S \to \neg R$} \RightLabel{$\; {\to}E$} \BIC{$\neg R$} \RightLabel{$\; {\wedge}I$} \BIC{$R \wedge \neg R$} \RightLabel{$\; {\neg}I_1$} \UIC{$\neg R$} \end{prooftree} \]

    In a natural deduction proof, every step needs to be justified by one of the rules.

    Example 2

    \[ \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$S \vee \neg S$} \AXC{$[\neg S]$} \AXC{$[R]$} \AXC{$R \to S$} \RightLabel{$\;{\to} E$} \BIC{$S$} \RightLabel{$\;{\wedge} I$} \BIC{$S \wedge \neg S$} \RightLabel{$\;{\neg} I$} \UIC{$\neg R$} \AXC{$[S]$} \AXC{$S \to \neg R$} \RightLabel{$\;{\to} E$} \BIC{$\neg R$} \RightLabel{$\;{\vee} E$} \TIC{$\neg R$} \end{prooftree} \]

    More examples of natural deduction proofs

    Example 4: anything follows from a contradiction

    Here are two proofs that \(\quad A, \; \neg A \vdash B\)

    Notice in both proofs the assumption \(\neg B\), the negation of the desired conclusion, is introduced into a conjunction, is then immediately eliminated, and is discharged at the end. We do not actually need the assumption of \(\neg B\) to derive the contradiction \(A \wedge \neg A\), but these examples are instructive in showing how the rules work as tools for manipulating formulas.

    \[ \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$A$} \AXC{$[\neg B]_1$} \RightLabel{$\; {\wedge} I$} \BIC{$A \wedge \neg B$} \RightLabel{$\; {\wedge} Er$} \UIC{$A$} \AXC{$\neg A$} \RightLabel{$\; {\wedge} I$} \BIC{$A \wedge \neg A$} \RightLabel{$\; {\neg} E_1$} \UIC{$B$} \end{prooftree} \qquad \qquad \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$A$} \AXC{$\neg A$} \RightLabel{$\; {\wedge} I$} \BIC{$A \wedge \neg A$} \AXC{$[\neg B]_1$} \RightLabel{$\; {\wedge} I$} \BIC{$(A \wedge \neg A) \wedge \neg B$} \RightLabel{$\; {\wedge} Er$} \UIC{$A \wedge \neg A$} \RightLabel{$\; {\neg} E_1$} \UIC{$B$} \end{prooftree} \]

    These examples have been includes as it is easy to see how they fit the pattern of the rule \({\neg}E\) where you can see the conclusion \(B\) together with the discharged assumption \(\neg B\).

    However, the rule \({\neg}E\) allows you to use an assumption such as \(\neg B\) any number of times, including zero. The same holds for the discharge of assumptions in other rules. This means that we can have the following proof that \(A, \; \neg A \vdash B\).

    \[ \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$A$} \AXC{$\neg A$} \RightLabel{$\; {\wedge} I$} \BIC{$A \wedge \neg A$} \RightLabel{$\; {\neg} E$} \UIC{$B$} \end{prooftree} \]

    Example 5

    Proof that \(\quad A \vee B, \; \neg A \vdash B\)

    Here the proof in the previous example appears as part of the larger proof.

    In this example we assume we have both \(A \vee B\) and \(\neg A\) then argue that \(B\) must hold. The pattern of the argument is that since \(A\) or \(B\) we can consider these two cases separately. In the case that \(A\) holds, then because we are assuming \(\neg A\) we have a contradiction from which anything can be derived. In particular we can derive \(B\). In the second of the two cases we immediately have that \(B\) holds. Since we have analysed both cases and found \(B\) in each case, we know \(B\) holds without any temporary assumption about which of the two cases holds.

    \[ \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$A \vee B$} \AXC{$[A]_1$} \AXC{$\neg A$} \RightLabel{$\; {\wedge} I$} \BIC{$A \wedge \neg A$} \RightLabel{$\; {\neg} E$} \UIC{$B$} \AXC{$[B]_1$} \RightLabel{$\; {\vee} E_1$} \TIC{$B$} \end{prooftree} \]

    Example 6

    Proof that \(\quad A \to B, \neg B \vdash \neg A\)

    \[ \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$[A]_1$} \AXC{$A \to B$} \RightLabel{$\; {\to} E$} \BIC{$B$} \AXC{$\neg B$} \RightLabel{$\; {\wedge} I$} \BIC{$B \wedge \neg B$} \RightLabel{$\; {\neg} I_1$} \UIC{$\neg A$} \end{prooftree} \]

    You should be able to take this example and demonstrate the principle of reasoning by contraposition. This is the principle that \(A \to B\) and \(\neg B \to \neg A\) are equivalent statements. In terms of proofs this means demonstrating both \(A \to B \vdash \neg B \to \neg A\) and \(\neg B \to \neg A \vdash A \to B\).

    Negated disjunction examples: 7 and 8

    Proof that \(\quad \neg (A \vee B) \vdash \neg A\)

    The strategy here should be familiar from previous examples. We want to conclude \(\neg A\), so we assume \(A\) and manage to show that this leads to a contradiction.

    \[ \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$[A]_1$} \RightLabel{$\; {\vee} Ir$} \UIC{$A \vee B$} \AXC{$\neg(A \vee B)$} \RightLabel{$\; {\wedge} I$} \BIC{$(A \vee B) \wedge \neg ( A \vee B)$} \RightLabel{$\; \neg I_1$} \UIC{$\neg A$} \end{prooftree} \]

    The fact that from \(\neg(A \vee B)\) we can deduce \(\neg A\) is useful in some proofs we build later. To avoid having to write the whole proof again, we will call the derived rule \({\neg}{\vee}Er\). Written in the same format as the fundamental rules it looks like this:

    \[\begin{prooftree} \AXC{$\neg(A \vee B)$} \RightLabel{$\;\;{\neg}{\vee}Er$} \UIC{$\neg A$} \end{prooftree} \]

    Proof that \(\quad \neg (A \vee B) \vdash \neg B\)

    \[ \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$[B]_1$} \RightLabel{$\; {\vee} I\ell$} \UIC{$A \vee B$} \AXC{$\neg(A \vee B)$} \RightLabel{$\; {\wedge} I$} \BIC{$(A \vee B) \wedge \neg ( A \vee B)$} \RightLabel{$\; \neg I_1$} \UIC{$\neg B$} \end{prooftree} \]

    Just as in the previous example, we can make a derived rule from this so that it can be reused easily.

    \[ \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$\neg(A \vee B)$} \RightLabel{$\;\;{\neg}{\vee}E\ell$} \UIC{$\neg B$} \end{prooftree} \]

    Example 9: double negation elimination

    Proof that \(\quad \neg \neg A \vdash A, \quad\)
    In this case the proof as it might be written in a less symbolic way is shown alongside.

    \[ \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$[\neg A]_1$} \AXC{$\neg \neg A$} \RightLabel{$\;\;{\wedge}I$} \BIC{$\neg A \wedge \neg \neg A$} \RightLabel{$\;\;{\neg}E_1$} \UIC{$A$} \end{prooftree} \qquad \begin{array}{l} \mathsf{We~ are~ given}\; \neg \neg A,\; \mathsf{suppose~ also~ that~ }\neg A.\\ \mathsf{Then~ we~ have~ both}\; \neg A\; \mathsf{and} \;\neg \neg A.\\ \mathsf{That~ is~ a~ contradiction;~ so} \; \neg A\; \mathsf{cannot~ hold,}\\ \mathsf{ and~ we~ must~ have~} A. \end{array} \]
    \[ \begin{array}{l} \mathsf{This~ makes~ another~ useful~ derived~ rule.}\\ \mathsf{It~ is~ called~ Double~ Negation~ Elimination,}\\ \mathsf{which~ we~ abbreviate~ as~ DNE.} \end{array} \qquad \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$\neg \neg A$} \RightLabel{$\;\;\mathsf{DNE}$} \UIC{$A$} \end{prooftree} \]

    Example 10: double negation introduction

    Proof that \(\quad A \vdash \neg \neg A \quad\) This is not as much use in practice as the elimination of double negation (\(\mathsf{DNE}\)). However, you should notice how the proof of the ability to introduce double negation has a very similar structure. In this case we use the negation introduction rule (\({\neg}I\)); to establish \(\mathsf{DNE}\) we used negation elimination (\({\neg}E\)).

    \[ \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$A$} \AXC{$[\neg A]_1$} \RightLabel{$\;\;{\wedge}I$} \BIC{$ A \wedge \neg A$} \RightLabel{$\;\;{\neg}I_1$} \UIC{$\neg \neg A$} \end{prooftree} \qquad \begin{array}{l} \mathsf{We~ are~ given}\; A,\; \mathsf{suppose~ also~ that~ }\neg A.\\ \mathsf{Then~ we~ have~ both}\; A\; \mathsf{and} \;\neg A.\\ \mathsf{That~ is~ a~ contradiction;~ so} \; \neg A\; \mathsf{cannot~ hold,}\\ \mathsf{ and~ we~ must~ have~} \neg \neg A. \end{array} \]

    Example 11: excluded middle (EM)

    Proof that \(\quad \vdash A \vee \neg A\)

    Excluded middle is so called because it expresses the idea that a logical statement is either true or its negation is true, and there is no possibility in the middle between these extremes. Excluded middle is sometimes called by the Latin name tertium non datur, which means that there is no third option.

    The idea behind the proof is to use contradiction. We assume that \(\neg(A \vee \neg A)\) holds. We then use the earlier proofs which gave the derived rules \({\neg}{\vee}Er\) and \({\neg}{\vee}E\ell\). These allow us to deduce both \(\neg A\) and \(\neg \neg A\). This is a contradiction: both \(\neg A\) and its negation. So our original assumption is impossible and we conclude that \(A \vee \neg A\) holds.

    \[ \begin{prooftree} \AXC{$[\neg(A \vee \neg A)]_1$} \RightLabel{$\;\;{\neg}{\vee}Er$} \UIC{$\neg A$} \AXC{$[\neg(A \vee \neg A)]_1$} \RightLabel{$\;\;{\neg}{\vee}E\ell$} \UIC{$ \neg \neg A$} \RightLabel{$\;\;{\wedge}I$} \BIC{$\neg A \wedge \neg \neg A$} \RightLabel{$\;\;{\neg}E_1$} \UIC{$A \vee \neg A$} \end{prooftree} \]

    Formative exercise

    Below are some suggestions for further practice in constructing natural deduction proofs.

    Solutions will be provided for all of these in Unit 4. If you have any questions before then, you can raise them in this week's webinar. In some cases there may be alternative solutions that are equally correct, so do not assume that if your answer is different then it is wrong.

    In each case you should construct a proof using the rules including labels for all steps so that you say which rule has been used and which assumptions (if any) have been discharged.

    1. \(\vdash A \to A\).
    2. \(B \vdash A \to B\).
    3. \(\vdash B \to (A \to B\)).
    4. These two to prove are already suggested in lesson 2 example 6: \(A \to B \vdash \neg B \to \neg A\) and \(\neg B \to \neg A \vdash A \to B\).
    5. \((A \to B) \to A \vdash B \to A\).
    6. \(A \wedge B \vdash A \to B\).
    7. \((A \to B) \to C \vdash A \to (B \to C)\).
    8. \(A \vee B \vdash (A \to B) \vee (B \to A)\).
    9. \(A \wedge B \vdash (A \to B) \wedge (B \to A)\).
    10. \(\neg A \vdash A \to B\).
