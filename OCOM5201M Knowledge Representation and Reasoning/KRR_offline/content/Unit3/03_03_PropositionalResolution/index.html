    Propositional resolution

    Learning outcomes:

    After completing this lesson you should be able to:

    • convert questions about deduction in propositional logic into clausal form so that resolution can be applied

    • identify all possible ways to resolve one clause with another

    • carry out the resolution refutation procedure on a given set of clauses.

    Introduction to resolution

    Download transcript (PDF).

    Literals

    A literal is a wff which is either an atom (an atomic proposition) or the negation of an atom.

    Atoms are also called positive literals and negated atoms are called negative literals.

    This does not mean literals have to be a single letter. The wff \(\mathit{tuesday} \vee \mathit{friday}\) contains two literals.

    Examples

    Which of the following wffs are positive literals, which are negative literals, and which are not literals?

    1. \(a \vee b\)
    2. \(a\)
    3. \(\neg \neg b\)
    4. \(a \to \neg b\)
    5. \(\neg b\)
    6. \(b\)
    Select to reveal answers
    1. \(a \vee b\)     Not a literal
    2. \(a\)    A positive literal
    3. \(\neg \neg b\)    Not a literal (only allowed a single \(\neg\) in a negative literal)
    4. \(a \to \neg b\)    Not a literal
    5. \(\neg b\)    A negative literal
    6. \(b\)    A positive literal

    Clauses

    A clause is a set of literals. There are lots of different notations, but clauses will be denoted with square brackets. For example \([a, \neg a, b]\) and \([a, \neg b]\) are clauses.

    The reason for not writing these sets with { and } is that we later need sets of clauses and we will use { and } for those.

    A clause is used to stand for the disjunction of its elements. For example the clause \([a, \neg b]\) is a useful way of referring to both the equivalent (but different) wffs \(a \vee \neg b\) and \(\neg b \vee a\). The clause \([a, b, c]\) refers to several different wffs including \(a \vee (b \vee c)\), and \((a \vee b) \vee c\), and \(b \vee (a \vee c)\).

    The empty clause, \([\,]\) is allowed and is equivalent to false.

    Horn clauses and prolog

    Horn clauses are clauses with at most one positive literal. In the table below, since \(A \to B\) is equivalent to \(\neg A \vee B\), the Horn clauses on the left can be expressed as the wffs in the second column. Their Prolog expression appears in the third column.

    \[ \begin{array}{l|l|l|l} \mathsf{Horn ~clause}& \mathsf{wff}& \mathsf{Prolog~ expression } & \mathsf{this~is~a}\\ \hline a \vee \neg b & b \to a & \texttt{ a :- b.} & \mathsf{rule}\\ \hline a & \mathsf{true} \to a & \texttt{ a.} & \mathsf{fact}\\ \hline \neg b \vee \neg a \vee c & (b \wedge a ) \to c & \texttt{ c :- b, a.} & \mathsf{rule}\\ \hline \neg a & a \to \mathsf{false} & \texttt{ :- a. } & \mathsf{goal}\\ \hline \end{array} \]

    Prolog implements resolution refutation.

    E.g. try to prove the goal \(a\) from statements \(b\), \(b \to a\).

    b.

    a :- b.

    :- a.

    You can put that in one file, but more usually you would have a file with the statements:

    b.

    a :- b.

    and then at the interactive prompt ?- type a

    Clausal resolution

    Resolution is an inference rule that lies at the heart of Prolog and many other systems for automating deduction. Here we give the propositional case of resolution.

    The following rule is applicable when \(\alpha\) is an atom, and \(\Sigma_1\) and \(\Sigma_2\) are clauses.

    \[ \frac{[\alpha] \cup \Sigma_{1} \quad[\neg \alpha] \cup \Sigma_{2}}{\Sigma_{1} \cup \Sigma_{2}} \text { resolution } \]

    The atom \(\alpha\) is called the atom resolved upon and the clause \(\Sigma_1 \cup \Sigma_2\) is the resolvent of the clauses \([\alpha]\cup \Sigma_1\) and \([\neg \alpha] \cup \Sigma_2\).

    Examples

    Where possible, carry out resolution on these pairs of clauses.

    1. \([a, b]\) and \([\neg b, c]\)
    2. \([a]\) and \([\neg a, b]\)
    3. \([a, b, c, d]\) and \([\neg a, b, e]\)
    4. \([a, b, \neg c]\) and \([a, \neg b, c, d]\)
    5. \([a]\) and \([\neg a]\)
    6. \([a, \neg a]\) and \([a, \neg a]\)
    7. \([a, \neg b]\) and \([b, \neg a]\)
    Select to reveal answers

    1. \([a,c]\)

    2. \([b]\)

    3. \([b,c,d,e]\)

    4. \([a,c,\neg c, d]\) and \([a,b,\neg b, d]\) can be obtained.

    5. \([\;]\)

    6. Nothing new can be obtained. You just get \([a, \neg a]\) again.

    7. \([a,\neg a]\) and \([b, \neg b]\) are obtained

    Conjunctive normal form

    Download transcript (PDF).

    Conjunctive Normal Form Resolution applies only to clauses. A clause is essentially a disjunction of literals, but not all wffs are of this form. What about \(a \to b\) or \(a \vee (b \wedge \neg a)\)? In fact every wff can be converted to an equivalent conjunction of clauses. This format is called CNF (Conjunctive Normal Form).

    Recall: equivalent means that in any given interpretation the two wffs will get the same truth value.

    Clausal form

    We saw CNF in unit 1 already, so we can translate a set of formulas into clausal form.

    We write

    \[ (a \vee b) \wedge (c \vee d) \wedge (a \vee d \vee e) \]

    in the form

    \[ \{\; [a, b],\; [c, d],\; [a, d, e]\;\} \]

    We can use a set of sets like this to represent any propositional formula in a convenient form.

    The innermost sets are treated as disjunctions of their elements, the whole is treated as the conjunction of its elements.

    \([\;]\) (empty disjunction) represents false, and

    \(\{\;\}\) (empty conjunction) represents true, and

    \(\{\;[\;]\;\}\) is a non-empty conjunction representing false.

    Resolution refutation procedure

    Download transcript (PDF).

    Given a finite set of wffs \(\Delta\) and a wff \(f\) carry out this procedure:

    1. Convert \(\Delta\) to CNF, giving a set of clauses, \(\Phi\).

    2. Convert the negation of \(f\) to CNF giving \(\Psi\).

    3. Let \(\Gamma = \Phi \cup \Psi\)

    4. Iteratively apply resolution to the clauses in \(\Gamma\) adding the resolvents to \(\Gamma\).

    Continue until no more resolution is possible or until the empty clause is a resolvent.

    Properties of the resolution rule

    Resolution is sound but not complete in the following sense:

    The statement 'resolution is sound' means that if we have any set \(\Delta\) of clauses and we use the resolution rule to build a proof of a clause \(f\) from \(\Delta\) then \(\Delta\) will logically entail \(f\).

    To justify the soundness of resolution, it's sufficient to show that if \([\alpha] \cup \Sigma_1\) and \([\neg \alpha] \cup \Sigma_2\) are true in some interpretation, then \(\Sigma_1 \cup \Sigma_2\) is also true in that interpretation.

    The resolution rule is not complete as \(a \wedge b\) logically entails \(a \vee b\) but we cannot take the clauses \([a], [b]\) and generate the clause \([a, b]\) by resolution.

    Properties of resolution refutation

    The properties of resolution refutation are:

    • If \(\Delta\) logically entails \(f\), then the procedure will generate the empty clause. This property is the completeness of resolution refutation.

    • If \(\Delta\) doesn't logically entail \(f\) then the procedure will terminate without producing the empty clause.

    See example \(\Delta=\{a \wedge b\}\), and \(f=a \vee b\) to understand the distinction between resolution rule alone and the resolution refutation procedure.

    Examples in resolution refutation

    Now you can try the resolution refutation procedure with the examples below:

    Remember \(\Delta\) is a set of formulas (a set of wffs), not a set of clauses, and \(f\) is also an arbitrary formula.

    1. \(\Delta = \{a \wedge b\}\), and \(f = a \vee b\).

    2. \(\Delta = \{a \to (b \vee c), a, \neg b\}\), and \(f = c\).

    3. \(\Delta = \{a \vee b\}\), and \(f = a \wedge b\).

    4. \(\Delta = \{ \}\), and \(f = a \to a\).

    5. \(\Delta = \{a \to b, b \to \neg a\}\), and \(f = a \wedge \neg a\).

    6. \(\Delta = \{a \wedge \neg b, b \to c\}\), and \(f = c\).

    Select to reveal answer to example 1

    • CNF for \(\Delta\); nothing to do here \(a \wedge b\) is already a conjunction of disjunctions of literals.

    • \(\Delta\) as clauses: \(\{[a],[b]\}\)

    • negate \(a \vee b\) to \(\neg(a \vee b)\)

    • put into CNF: \(\neg a \wedge \neg b\). As clauses: \(\{[\neg a], [\neg b]\}\)

    • \(\Gamma = \{[a],[b], [\neg a], [\neg b]\}\)

    • Resolve \([\neg a], [a]\) yields \([\;]\) empty clause

    • Result makes sense: if both \(a\) and \(b\) hold, then at least one of them does

    Select to reveal answer to example 2

    • CNF for \(\Delta\); we need to replace \(a \to (b \vee c)\) by \(\neg a \vee b \vee c\)

    • \(\Delta\) as clauses: \(\{[\neg a, b, c], [a], [\neg b]\}\)

    • negate \(c\) to \(\neg c\)

    • put into CNF and express as clauses: \(\{[\neg c]\}\)

    • \(\Gamma = \{[\neg a, b, c], [a], [\neg b], [\neg c]\}\)

    • Resolve \([\neg a, b, c], [a]\) yields \([b, c]\)

    • Resolve \([b, c], [\neg b]\) yields \([c]\)

    • Resolve \([c], [\neg c]\) yields \([\;]\)

    • Result makes sense: if \(a\) implies \(b\) or \(c\) but \(b\) does not hold, then \(c\) must.

    Select to reveal answer to example 3

    • CNF for \(\Delta\); nothing to do this time

    • \(\Delta\) as clauses: \(\{[a, b]\}\)

    • negate \(a \wedge b\) to \(\neg (a \wedge b)\)

    • put into CNF and express as clauses: \(\{[\neg a, \neg b]\}\)

    • \(\Gamma = \{[a, b], [\neg a, \neg b]\}\)

    • Resolve \([a, b], [\neg a, \neg b]\) yields \([b, \neg b]\)

    • Resolve \([a, b], [\neg a, \neg b]\) yields \([a, \neg a]\)

    • We end up with a set of clauses: \([a, b], [\neg a, \neg b], [b, \neg b], [a, \neg a]\) and nothing further can be generated. We conclude that \(a \vee b\) does not entail \(a \wedge b\).

    • Result makes sense: if we only know \(a\) or \(b\) then there is no reason why we must then know that both \(a\) and \(b\).

    Select to reveal answer to example 4

    • CNF for \(\Delta\); nothing to do this time as it is the empty set of formulas.

    • \(\Delta\) as a set of clauses: \(\{\;\}\). There are no clauses. Do not confuse this with having the empty clause.

    • negate \(a \to a\) to \(\neg (a \to a)\)

    • put into CNF: \(a \wedge \neg a\) and express as clauses: \(\{[a], [\neg a]\}\)

    • \(\Gamma = \{[a], [\neg a]\}\)

    • Resolve \([a], [\neg a]\) yields \([\;]\)

    • We got the empty clause, so the empty set of formulas entails \(a \to a\)

    • Result makes sense: \(a \to a\) is a tautology. It always holds and we need not make any assumptions to ensure it holds.

    Select to reveal answer to example 5

    • CNF for \(\Delta\); \(a \to b\) is \(\neg a \vee b\); \(b \to \neg a\) is \(\neg b \vee \neg a\)

    • \(\Delta\) as clauses: \(\{[\neg a, b],[\neg b, \neg a]\}\)

    • negate \(a \wedge \neg a\) to \(\neg(a \wedge \neg a)\)

    • put into CNF: \(\neg a \vee a\). As clauses: \(\{[\neg a, a]\}\)

    • \(\Gamma = \{[\neg a, b],[\neg b, \neg a], [\neg a, a]\}\)

    • Resolve \([\neg b, \neg a], [\neg a, a]\) yields \([\neg b, \neg a]\) (nothing new)

    • Resolve \([\neg a, b], [\neg b, \neg a]\) yields \([\neg a]\)

    • Nothing further can be generated: conclude not entailed

    • This is what we expect: \(\Delta\) is satisfiable (put \(a\) false) but \(a \wedge \neg a\) is a contradiction.

    Select to reveal answer to example 6

    • CNF for \(\Delta\); \(a \wedge \neg b\) is in CNF; \(b \to c\) is \(\neg b \vee c\)

    • \(\Delta\) as clauses: \(\{[a],[\neg b], [\neg b, c]\}\)

    • negate \(c\) to \(\neg c\); as clauses: \(\{[\neg c]\}\)

    • \(\Gamma = \{[a],[\neg b], [\neg b, c], [\neg c]\}\)

    • can only resolve \([\neg b, c]\) against \([\neg c]\)

    • we get \(\{[a],[\neg b], [\neg b, c], [\neg c]\}\) and can go no further

    • Conclude: formula is not entailed.
